\section{Principles}

Kungfu's product direction is grounded in the KFD principles.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
KFD-1 & facts must not drift. \\
KFD-2 & trust must start from facts. \\
KFD-3 & cooperation must start from trusted value. \\
KFD-4 & timelines must declare their observer. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

These principles are not slogans for marketing copy. They are release and
product constraints.

KFD-1 says that a product cannot rely on facts that silently drift between
development, build, packaging, runtime, and user-visible surfaces. For Kungfu,
this applies to configuration, schemas, agent interfaces, task ledgers, storage
formats, release evidence, and extension contracts.

KFD-2 says that users and agents need to know what kind of trust a surface
deserves. Some facts can be byte-verified. Some can be checked against a
release passport. Some are natural-language judgments and require residual
risk. A product that hides those distinctions asks users to trust complexity
without a path to inspect it.

KFD-3 says that cooperation begins when another participant can see the value
and boundary of using the product. This applies to humans and agents. Kungfu
should not force agents into brittle rituals. It should expose transparent
mechanisms that let agents understand why using Kungfu helps them complete
work.

KFD-4 says that complex timelines should not pretend to be a view from nowhere.
In real systems, the important question is often not whether one global clock
has absolute truth. It is whether an observer's declared view is stable,
auditable, and honest about what it has not seen.

Together these principles create a practical trust model: stable facts,
inspectable trust, transparent cooperation, and observer-declared timelines.
